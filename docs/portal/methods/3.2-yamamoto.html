Yamamoto isotherm fit on packed-bed affinity columns

How DPSim resolves the residence-time vs. capacity tradeoff under explicit uncertainty propagation, and what an evidence tier means at every step of the fit.

DPSim 0.8.9 commit 6642532 CALIBRATED · LOCAL

The dynamic binding capacity of an affinity column is the load you can apply before breakthrough — the moment the protein you wanted to capture starts coming out the other end. It is the single most important number in packed-bed chromatography, and it is the easiest one to lie to yourself about. DPSim refuses to render it without an evidence tier¹, and the renderer is architecturally biased toward silence over false precision.

The isotherm we fit is Yamamoto's two-parameter form, parameterised by maximum capacity q_max and the binding affinity constant K. The fit is performed under the lumped-rate transport model with a BDF solver when the gradient program is time-varying, LSODA otherwise². The two solver branches exist because LSODA oscillates modes when the binding equilibrium is non-stationary; this was diagnosed in v0.7.4 and is now the documented behaviour.

q = (qmax · K · c) / (1 + K · c) (3.2.1)

Equation (3.2.1) collapses, in the dilute limit (K·c ≪ 1), to q ≈ qmax·K·c — a linear regime where the apparent affinity K dominates and the column is far from saturation. The opposite limit (K·c ≫ 1) gives q → qmax: the column is loaded to its ceiling and additional protein flows straight through. Calibrated fits live in the curved transition region where neither limit dominates and where small errors in K propagate hardest into capacity estimates.

What the evidence tier means here

A CALIBRATED badge means we have wet-lab calibration data for the specific reagent / ligand / matrix combination at hand — typical case for BSA on Protein A agarose³. SEMI-QUANT. means we are extrapolating from a related family; the breakthrough time has the right order of magnitude but you should not cite it as a final number. QUAL. means we are predicting only the direction of change, never the value.

The tier flows from the input data, not from the fit quality. A perfect R² = 0.999 on uncalibrated extrapolation still ships as SEMI-QUANT. The fit answers "is this self-consistent?" The tier answers "is this connected to reality?" These are independent questions, and DPSim refuses to conflate them.

No quantitative claim leaves DPSim without a tier. If the renderer cannot decide, the result is suppressed and the user is told why. The simulator is architecturally biased toward silence over false precision — a posture inherited from analytical chemistry, not from data dashboards.

Limits of the fit

The Yamamoto two-parameter fit assumes monolayer adsorption with no cooperativity. For Protein A agarose with BSA in the calibrated range, that assumption holds to within experimental error. Three regimes break it. First, multilayer effects at very high c — out of scope for affinity capture but visible in ion-exchange. Second, multivalent ligand interactions with antibody fragments — DPSim downgrades these fits to SEMI-QUANT. automatically. Third, mass-transport limitations at high linear velocity, where the fitted K apparent decreases with flowrate; we surface this as a velocity-dependence warning in the run footer and refuse to extrapolate beyond ±20% of the calibration velocity.

The architecture choice we keep getting asked about is why the fit lives in module3_performance/orchestrator.py rather than in a dedicated isotherm module. The answer is that a fitted isotherm is not a standalone object in DPSim; it is an attribute of a run, scoped to the (reagent, ligand, matrix, velocity) tuple under which calibration was performed. Lifting it out of the orchestrator would force a parallel scope-tracking layer that the orchestrator already provides for free.
